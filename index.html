CHAPTER ONE

CALVIN'S JOURNEY

CCalvin Kirby, a 34 year old man from Gallipolis, Ohio, was raised in an abusive household. To tell the story of Sobriety Point, one must understand why its creator, Calvin, needed it in the first place.

Growing up, Calvin experienced a series of traumas that he says contributed to his drug addiction. His mother, who was separated from his father, was addicted to drugs and moved around frequently. Along with living in an unstable household, This left him feeling isolated and in need of help. The only way he knew how to relieve his pain was from using.

What led us to being homeless at first? Drug activity and drug addiction.”
-Calvin Kirby

(00:29)

Calvin and Angel Kirby married June 20, 2011. Calvin was using when they first met and Angel was in recovery, but soon after she relapsed. Calvin says he had a temper that made him unbearable for others to be around him, which ultimately led him and Angel to homelessness. Calvin needed to be in nature, away from the stresses of society. Frustrated with life on the streets in Parkersburg, West Virginia, the Kirbys found an unoccupied plot of land that is owned by the city. Tucked away between a bustling highway and quiet creek that branches off of the Ohio River, Sobriety Point’s juxtaposition provided a sense of privacy and comfort that most homeless don't get to experience on the streets in larger cities. Calvin when high once received a tattoo with his last name spelt wrong across his back. It’s something that he will forever have as a reminder of what drugs can do to him.

CHAPTER TWO

SOBRIETY POINT

D

riving westbound on Route 50, smoke arises from unseen chimneys. Wood-burning stoves fleetingly peak over the metal railing and are often missed by the naked eye.

Sobriety Point is a sober homeless camp where residents live a sober life. Removed from the homeless camps that don’t often encourage sober living environments, creators Calvin and Angel Kirby built Sobriety Point for themselves to live. They now live in a home and have full-time jobs; yet, the camp still stands and is open to residents willing to be sober.
